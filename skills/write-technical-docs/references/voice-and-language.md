# Voice and language

Use this reference when drafting or reviewing prose, or when accessibility, inclusion, or localization affects the content.

## Voice and tone

- Sound like a knowledgeable, respectful colleague. Be conversational without slang, cuteness, hype, or excessive formality.
- Put useful information first. Avoid throat-clearing, placeholder phrases, unnecessary apologies, repeated politeness, and exclamation marks.
- Address the reader as **you**. Use **we** only for the organization or author when that actor matters; do not use **we** to mean the reader and writer together.
- Prefer direct imperatives in procedures. Do not add *please* to routine instructions or use *let's* to lead the reader through a task.
- Do not describe a process as *easy*, *simple*, *quick*, or *obvious*. These claims do not help the reader and can dismiss real difficulty.
- Avoid unsupported superlatives, competitive claims, promises, and statements about unreleased features.

## Clear sentences

- Prefer active voice and name the actor. Use passive voice when the actor is irrelevant, the result deserves emphasis, or naming the actor would blame the reader unnecessarily.
- Use present tense for current behavior. Use future tense only for an actual future event or result.
- Put a condition, circumstance, or goal before its instruction: “To delete the file, select **Delete**.”
- Keep the subject and verb near the beginning. Use common subject–verb–object order.
- Express one main idea per sentence. Aim for sentences shorter than 26 words, but preserve necessary precision.
- Prefer familiar, precise words. Replace inflated phrases with shorter equivalents when meaning stays intact.
- Avoid noun stacks, ambiguous modifiers, double negatives, and a series of exceptions to exceptions.
- Use helper words such as *that*, *then*, and *of* when omitting them could create ambiguity.
- Make every pronoun's antecedent clear. Repeat the noun when a reader or translator might otherwise guess.
- Use a term consistently for one concept. Do not vary terminology merely to avoid repetition.

## Technical terms and abbreviations

- Use a technical term when it is more precise than plain language. Define unfamiliar terms close to first use.
- Spell out an abbreviation on first use unless the audience is certain to know it or the short form is the official name.
- Avoid jargon when a common term works. If the reader must recognize both forms, introduce the preferred term and mention the alternative once.
- Add a qualifying noun when a code token alone would be grammatically unclear, such as “the `settings.yaml` file.”
- Avoid anthropomorphism that obscures behavior. Name what a service, function, or system actually does.

## Global audience

- For English documentation, use US English unless project rules specify otherwise.
- Use short, literal, unambiguous language that can be translated without cultural interpretation.
- Avoid idioms, metaphors, humor, slang, pop-culture references, region-specific holidays, sports references, and seasons as time markers.
- Avoid phrasal verbs when a simple single verb is equally natural and precise.
- Use unambiguous dates, times, units, and examples. Do not assume a locale, time zone, currency, address format, or measurement system.
- Keep repeated phrases, terms, capitalization, and formatting consistent so translation memory can recognize them.
- Do not encode essential wording in screenshots or figures; images are difficult to localize.

## Inclusive language

- Use gender-neutral language unless a person's stated gender is relevant. Use singular **they** when a pronoun is needed for a person of unknown or irrelevant gender.
- Avoid ableist, violent, divisive, dehumanizing, or unnecessarily gendered metaphors.
- Describe people neutrally and according to the terminology preferred by the community in question. Research preferences when identity is relevant.
- Use diverse, culturally plausible names and locations across a set of examples without turning identity into decoration.
- Replace non-inclusive legacy terminology in prose. If it is an exact code identifier or established term that readers must recognize, format it as code or mention it once, then use a neutral descriptive term.
- Do not describe people without disabilities as *normal* or describe a disability through pity, euphemism, or assumed suffering.

## Accessible prose

- Make the page scannable with descriptive headings, short paragraphs, and parallel lists.
- Put the distinguishing point in the first sentence of a paragraph.
- Do not rely on punctuation, capitalization, font treatment, color, size, direction, or position to convey meaning.
- Refer to controls by their accessible label, not their color, shape, or location.
- Avoid spatial references such as *above*, *below*, *left*, or *right*. Refer to a named section, figure, or preceding/following element.
- Write meaningful link text that works when read alone.
- Supply equivalent text for information in images, audio, video, or interactive elements.
